Chen Duxiu vs. Mao Zedong

Using data to understand their different visions for communist China

by Aneesh Pappu

History 98

Tom Mullaney

What's my final project?

I looked at a dataset of 'national material capability' that included data on urban population, total population, and iron and steel production (i.e. industrial activity) from 1860-2007.
A major occurrence in the communist party was Chen Duxiu and Mao Zedong's ideological split -- specifically, Chen's criticism of Mao's agrarian flavor of communism. Using this data to understand the breakdown of Chinese class at the time, and exploring further comparisons with other relevant nations, I became interested in the factors that led to Chen Duxiu's strong preference for industrial-focused communism over Mao's agrarian style, given contrasting data in China.

The Data

I started by looking at industrial activity between China, Japan, Russia, and other countries that China has had significant relations with. I noticed that from 1900 to 1917, China's iron and steel production was stagnant when compared to multiple of its neighbors. Situating this in juxtaposition with the fall of the Qing and the initial formation of the Communist party, I started to wonder how Chen and his early co-conspirators formed their original ideas of communism, and whether these trends could be related.
Below is iron and steel production (an indicator of industrial activity) plotted for both China and Russia from 1905 (approximately when Chen was initially exposed to Marxism, shortly after he left for Japan in 1902) and 1917 (when the Russian Revolution occurred, a large inspiration in the formation of the Chinese Communist Party). I solely compared Russia and China because Chen is said to have been heavily influenced by the Russian Revolution.

Iron and Steel Production in Russia and China

Chen was heavily inspired by Marxist-Leninist communism which placed heavy emphasis on the industrial class. The application of Marxist-Leninist thought in Russia, from whom Chen and his early co-conspirators that formed the Chinese Communist Party gained much inspiration, made sense in the context of its rapidly rising industrial activity as shown in the graph above. However, China's industrial activity is clearly stagnant (and nearly nonexistent) during this time period when Chen is structuring his earliest ideas of communism. This stark difference started to beg the question of how Chen became confident that Marxist-Leninist thought, in its original industrially-focused form, could be applied to China.
Delving deeper, Chen Duxiu's disagreement with Mao Zedong in 1925 was primarily revolving around the role of the agrarian vs industrial worker classes in communist revolution. Below is a graph of the percent of total population that is urban between 1920 (earliest available data and before Chen Duxiu's conflict with Mao) and 1930 (shortly after Chen is expelled from the party in 1929), plotted across China, Russia (the communist 'older brother'), and Germany (the birthplace of Marx and a cornerstone of Marx's focus on the industrial class).

Percent Urban Population out of Total Population in China, Russia, and Germany

Mao's agrarian form of communism seems more grounded in reality -- compared to Russia and Germany, China's urban population percentage is near stagnant. The contrast between Russia's rapidly growing urban population and Germany's already high percentage of urban population with China's stagnant urban population further begs the question of why Chen refuted Mao's claim of agrarian primacy in revolution.

Final Paper

My final research proposal delves into this question. Specifically, "what factors caused Chen Duxiu to believe in the Marxist-Leninist industrial model of revolution more than Mao?".
